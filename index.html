Frequently Asked Questions

Get clear answers to common legal queries

How can I book a legal consultation?

+

You can book a consultation by filling out the consultation form on this website or by contacting us directly via phone or WhatsApp. All consultations are handled confidentially.

Do you handle cases before the High Court of Andhra Pradesh?

+

Yes. I actively practice before the High Court of Andhra Pradesh, handling writ petitions, bail matters, service cases, civil disputes, and other High Court matters.

What types of cases do you handle?

+

I provide legal representation in:

  • Writ Petitions
  • Bail Matters (Anticipatory & Regular)
  • Service & Employment Matters
  • Family Law Matters
  • Civil & Property Disputes
  • Municipal & Panchayat Matters

How long does a High Court case usually take?

+

The duration depends on the nature and complexity of the case. Some matters receive interim relief quickly, while others may take longer depending on court schedules and legal procedures.

Do you handle urgent matters like bail and interim relief?

+

Yes. Urgent matters such as bail applications, interim orders, and stay petitions are handled with priority, subject to court availability.

Is my information kept confidential?

+

Absolutely. All client information and case details are treated with strict confidentiality, in accordance with professional legal ethics.

What documents should I bring for consultation?

+

You should bring:

  • Relevant case papers or notices
  • Court orders (if any)
  • Identification documents

This helps in providing accurate legal advice.

Do you provide honest advice even if the case is weak?

+

Yes. I believe in honest and ethical legal practice. Clients are given clear and practical advice based on law and facts, without false assurances.

How are legal fees decided?

+

Fees depend on the nature, complexity, and urgency of the case. All fee structures are explained clearly in advance with transparency.

Can existing court orders be challenged or modified?

+

Yes. Depending on the facts and applicable law, existing orders can be challenged, reviewed, or modified through appropriate legal proceedings.
